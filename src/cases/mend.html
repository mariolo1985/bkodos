Overview

Every day there are lots of families that have children staying in hospitals being treated for illnesses. Often, for extended amounts of time. Largely these children are taken care of due to the illnesses, although their other family members still have to sleep, eat, and take care of other necessities during this hard time. The main question is what can we do to help these families?

As students at DevMountain in Salt Lake City, my classmate Mike Curtis and I took on the challenge to find a way to help these families, in particular, the parents, during this emotionally and financially stressful time. Below are our design process and the role I played in the creation of our app, MEND.

Research

Assumptions

As a team, we came together to understand the issue at hand. Such as figuring out what we do know and what we don't about the situation. Along with some assumptions about the issue at hand.

Figuring out the questions we had about helping these families and applying the assumptions into action is where the research began.

Research

There was a lot of research on what resources were already available for this issue. We stumbled across this article http://www.caringvoice.org/2015/03/traveling-treatment/ in which discussed exactly that. They listed several organizations that help those in these hard situations, what they do, and why they do them.

Finding out that there were all of these organizations help solve issues such as:

  • Providing Room & Board
  • Providing Emotional Support
  • Sometimes providing free medical care

This discovery really helped narrow down Ideas of what we could do. The next step in the process that really helped was the Interviews.

Interviews

Contextual Inquiry Interviews

The interviews provided great insight on how the families felt and what they were going through on a daily basis. As observer and note taker, I was able to really pay attention to body language, take in the environment around affected families, which was extremely important information.

The interview process quickly taught us two important things:

  • They needed motivation throughout their day to keep them going
  • They had a lot of things to get done at home, yet had a hard time asking for help

Persona

Armed with the knowledge of the research and interviews we were now able to formulate our Main Persona for the app.

Creative Brief

With all this, we had more of an idea of the overall plan of what we wanted to accomplish, and with this knowledge, we were able to create our Creative Brief.

Create a User Story Map, User Flow & Sitemap

The combination of all of our research helped us come up with an idea, and with that idea, we wanted to map it out. How it would flow, and what the sitemap would look like.

10×10 Method & Sketching Phase:

With more and more information figured out now came the fun part. Putting pen to paper we did a series of 10x10 sketches for each screen to help figure out the final design for each screen Mend needs.

After choosing the designs we liked we sketched final versions to put in our sketch-book so we can refer to them in the wireframing phase.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes:

Final UI & Hand Off

Implement a Style Guide:

A project like Mend really starts to hit home once you see the pieces coming together. We started to develop a style guide for the app, including colors, font choices, grid layouts, and copy for the app. We spent an afternoon sketching out ideas for the Mend logo, and ultimately settled on the logo you see throughout this case study. It utilizes a simple font and subtly embodies the concept of sharing with the three dots in the typical sharing icon positioning.

Hi-Fi Mock-Ups:

With our style guide in place, we could begin putting the skin on the app. We chose Android as our development platform and did our best to adhere to Google’s Material Design standards for color, sizing, objects, text, and more. It was undoubtedly a learning process, but we soon realized that within the inherent, restrictive guidelines of Material Design, it also exposed you to a whole lot of freedom, possibilities, and fluidity in design.

Conduct Usability Testing

Our initial tests showed an accessibility color issue. Our color scheme had to be tweaked slightly because we wanted to accommodate those with colorblindness.

Ideate, Refine, Test Again

We adjusted some of our screens in Sketch based on the feedback we received and updated our InVision prototype to reflect those changes.

Project Impact

Two powerful insights kept surfacing during the five weeks that we had to complete this project.

  • First: We realized that this app could be used for anyone going through a difficult time. Instead of just parents with children in pediatric care, what about families dealing with the loss of a loved one? What about someone who just went through surgery and needs help accomplishing tasks around the house? What about elderly folks? What if you get hurt at work and need some things done around the house? We now realize that the goal of this app is not to be just another to-do app, but to encourage people to create a task, then ask for help in getting them done.
  • Second: We have become emotionally attached to this project! For me, this happened during the research and interview phase. Sitting down with those moms and listening to their struggles tugged at my heartstrings. I found myself staying up late coming up with ideas and refining our current ones. My commitment to this project was like nothing I had experienced before and this was exciting! I look forward to having a working product in hand that we can hand to these moms, and others across the world, to help them during their most difficult times.
If UX is about people and solving real-world problems, then this project epitomizes user experience design beautifully. As we move towards the development phase of this project, I hope to soon have a finished, polished product to put into the hands of the moms we interviewed.
